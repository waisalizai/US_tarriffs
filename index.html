How will Trump trade wars fuel inflation globally?

US President Donald Trump has imposed 10% baseline tariffs on imports from all countries, undermining efforts to reboot economic growth and combat inflation globally.

President Donald Trump signing executive orders. Image Credit: SHEALAH CRAIGHEAD

By Wais Ahmad Alizai 9 April 2025

Trump has so far not shown any signs of retreating from his policy of levying high tariffs on almost all the US trade partners. If his tit-for-tat tariffs continue with China, most direct trade ties between two economic powers will likely come to an end.

Yet, Trump is not steadfast when it comes to tariffs as his administration has excluded “smartphones and other consumer electronics from its steep ‘reciprocal’ tariffs in a significant boost for Big Tech.”

Meanwhile, a report by the Guardian newspaper quoted Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) – which represents the richest countries – as saying that the fallout from higher trade barriers and mounting geopolitical uncertainty were undermining the recent higher levels of economic growth and progress to bring down inflation.

The OECD’s warning sent an alarm that tariffs can trigger more tit-for-tat measures which may push up prices for consumers globally

Although President Trump levied the lowest tariffs on the UK compared to other countries, British Secretary of State for Business and Trade Jonathan Reynolds said that he still viewed the greater trade barriers as "disappointing".

However, some economists believe that tariffs may support specific domestic industries in the short term, but they usually end up in higher prices for consumers.

It is said that when the US sneezes, the entire world catches cold. The baseline tariff and more importantly, 25% tariffs will have significant and severe impacts on luxury cars. If governments, especially the British government, does not step in, staff at UK high-end car plants will lose jobs.

Tariffs would increase UK-made car prices

The UK Department for Business and Trade said in a factsheet that the total UK exports to US amounted to £182.6 billion in the four quarters to the end of Q3 2024, with the total UK imports from US amounting to £111.5 billion over the same period.

“Total UK exports to United States amounted to £182.6 billion in the four quarters to the end of Q3 2024 (a decrease of 0.5% or £889 million in current prices, compared to the four quarters to the end of Q3 2023)”, said the factsheet.

As the tariffs are in the beginning stage, it is still uncertain to what extent, it would increase the UK-made cars' prices. But in fact, high-end cars are in the top five UK exports to the US. The automotive industry meanwhile warned of facing economic problems and price increases if the British government does not offer financial assistance such as furlough schemes .

Moreover, the Financial Times wrote in an article that those who look to buy a luxury car face paying thousands more as the global tariff war drives up the cost of prestigious vehicles.

“The concern for luxury-car enthusiasts in the UK and worldwide is that a Rolls-Royce or an Aston Martin could soon become far more expensive to buy if the global trade war is not brought to an end,” said the article.

Also, the article quoted Scott Sherwood, an independent analyst of supercars and luxury car brands, as saying that prices of high-end marques will rise for consumers outside of the US since car manufacturers will try to spread the financial hit across their entire market and customer base.

Bank of England increases interest rates to decrease inflation

As the inflation rate is above the 2% target, the Bank of England has increased the interest rates to 5.25% to help lower inflation.

A BBC report described the 5.25% increase as “a 16-year high”, arguing that if you make borrowing more expensive, people have less money to spend and more importantly, people are encouraged to save more. As a result, this decreases the demand for goods and slows price rises, according to the report.

Moreover, the Bank of England has halved its growth forecast for this year as it cut interest rates from 4.75% to 4.5% - the lowest level for more than 18 months, said another BBC report published on 6 February.

Bank of England. Image Credit: Wais Ahmad Alizai

Amid the cut in the interest rates, the economy is now expected to grow by 0.75% from its previous estimate of 1.5%. Indirectly pointing to Trump’s trade wars, Governor of the Bank of England Andrew Bailey told BBC that the bank expected to be able to cut rates further, “but we will have to judge meeting by meeting, how far and how fast.”

"We live in an uncertain world, and the road ahead will have bumps on it," the BBC report quoted him as adding.

What happens next?

The US tariffs will carry implications for the UK. US consumers would shift from buying UK-made products to domestic ones, as a result of which it will have negative impacts on the UK economy and will increase inflation.

“US consumers and firms switching spending from UK goods, reducing demand for UK exports and weighing on UK activity and inflation,” said House of Lords Library.

However, there is a scenario if the US keeps levying tariffs on countries, firms will move their investments to the UK in order to be in a better position to export to the US when it comes to tariffs.

The House of Lords Library further said: “If higher US tariffs are implemented and maintained for some time, firms in countries facing high reciprocal tariffs may relocate business activities to the UK to be better placed to export to the US. So the UK may see more foreign investment as a consequence.”

But the drawback is that the tariffs have arisen concerns that Europe, including UK, may turn into a place for China to dump its cheapest products.

Currys chief executive Alex Baldock told the Financial Times that there were early signs of “stock being diverted into European markets in a straightforward dumping way”.
